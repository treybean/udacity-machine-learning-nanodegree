Transforming Skewed Continuous Features¶

A dataset may sometimes contain at least one feature whose values tend to lie near a single number, but will also have a non-trivial number of vastly larger or smaller values than that single number. Algorithms can be sensitive to such distributions of values and can underperform if the range is not properly normalized. With the census dataset two features fit this description: 'capital-gain' and 'capital-loss'.

Run the code cell below to plot a histogram of these two features. Note the range of the values present and how they are distributed.

For highly-skewed feature distributions such as 'capital-gain' and 'capital-loss', it is common practice to apply a logarithmic transformation on the data so that the very large and very small values do not negatively affect the performance of a learning algorithm. Using a logarithmic transformation significantly reduces the range of values caused by outliers. Care must be taken when applying this transformation however: The logarithm of 0 is undefined, so we must translate the values by a small amount above 0 to apply the the logarithm successfully.

Metrics and the Naive Predictor¶

CharityML, equipped with their research, knows individuals that make more than \$50,000 are most likely to donate to their charity. Because of this, *CharityML* is particularly interested in predicting who makes more than \$50,000 accurately. It would seem that using accuracy as a metric for evaluating a particular model's performace would be appropriate. Additionally, identifying someone that does not make more than \$50,000 as someone who does would be detrimental to *CharityML*, since they are looking to find individuals willing to donate. Therefore, a model's ability to precisely predict those that make more than \$50,000 is more important than the model's ability to recall those individuals. We can use F-beta score as a metric that considers both precision and recall:

$$ F_{\beta} = (1 + \beta^2) \cdot \frac{precision \cdot recall}{\left( \beta^2 \cdot precision \right) + recall} $$

In particular, when $\beta = 0.5$, more emphasis is placed on precision. This is called the F$_{0.5}$ score (or F-score for simplicity).

Looking at the distribution of classes (those who make at most \$50,000, and those who make more), it's clear most individuals do not make more than \$50,000. This can greatly affect accuracy, since we could simply say "this person does not make more than \$50,000" and generally be right, without ever looking at the data! Making such a statement would be called naive, since we have not considered any information to substantiate the claim. It is always important to consider the naive prediction for your data, to help establish a benchmark for whether a model is performing well. That been said, using that prediction would be pointless: If we predicted all people made less than \$50,000, CharityML would identify no one as donors.

Decision Trees¶

Decision trees are a great model that can be used for both classification and regression problems, especially when the decision boundary function is likely not linear, or even continous for that matter. It also performs especially well for categorical data, often making data normalization and dummy variables unnecessary. They are also pretty fast, both to train and test with, making it a good choice for large data sets.

That said, there are some downsides to decision trees. Primarily, it seems that it's very easy to over-fit with decision trees by creating a decision boundary that maps near perfectly for your training data, but doesn't generalize well and therefore performs poorly when making predictions on your test data. Additionally, decision trees can be non-robust and have high variance, meaning a small change in your training data can result in large changes in the resulting tree and, therefore, your predictions. If the data is noisy, then a decision tree is very likely to perform poorly.

An example of someone using decision trees in a real-world application is using decision trees to analyze student data and predict a student's final GPA as well as make recommendations for courses in their study plan. See the paper Predicting Students Final GPA Using Decision Trees: A Case Study.

Decision Trees are a good candidate for this problem for lots of reasons: a) it's a binary classification problem in with the data is liklely not linearly separable. For example, it could devise a model that classifies a two 33 year-old, male, who work 10 hours a week both as making no more than \$50k/year and more than \$50k/year based on other features, e.g. capitcal gains. It feels like that would be difficult to achieve with a linear model. Also, since our features contain both numerical and categorical data, decision trees could be a good fit. We could even experiment backing out some of the dummy variables we created above. With decision trees being pretty fast at predicting, this is anothere compelling reason to consider them for this problem—once trained, we could process millions of new people in census data in a short time. Finally, decision trees are one of the models that very closely resembles human decision making, so it's very easy to explain to a client, like CharityML, in terms that will feel natural to them. It's not worth making the whole decision based on this, but if performance is close, being able to articulate your work to a client in terms they understand and can give them confidence is definitely worth consideration.

Ensemble Methods¶

While decision trees are a good option for the problem at hand, it is a supervised learning algorithm that yields just one hypothesis in the hypothesis space. Ensemble methods combine multiple hypothesises by a couple different strategies to increase the robustness or predictive power of the overall model, the primary strength of ensemble methods. Depending on the type of ensemble strategy being used, you can achieve models with lower variance—as is the case with averaging methods like bagging or Random Forest—or lower bias—as is the case with Boosting (AdaBoost and Gradient Boosting).

This increased accuracy comes at a cost, though. For many ensemble strategies, the training time is higher—at least in comparison to the training time of the same underlying single algorithm—and requires more resources. Aside from that, though, I can't think of many reasons why you wouldn't at least attempt to utilize ensemble methods in most supervised learning problems. It's possible that the data is just really nice and a single learner is able to produce a model with low variance and low bias. In that case, it could be a waste to utilize ensemble methods, but I think that would be rare.

An example of ensemble methods being used in the real-world is in the Netflix competition. The team that coule improve Netflix's recommendation system by 10% would win $1M. The team that came in third, Ensemble Experts, used ensemble methods heavily to do so well, https://wiki.eecs.yorku.ca/course_archive/2014-15/F/4412/_media/ensemble_data_mining.pdf.

Gaussian Naive Bayes¶

A Naive Bayes classifier is a supervised learning algorithm that leverages Bayes's theorem, which describes a relationship between the probability of an event based on the probabilities of other conditions related to the event. Naive Bayes makes an assumption that all of the conditions are independent of one another. Though, it's unlikely that that assumption holds true in the real world, it has, nonetheless, not proven to diminish the success of achieving high accuracy with this algorithm. Considering our problem at hand is trying to do just that—determine a probability that a given individual makes more than \$50k/year based on other conditions in the census data, it seems like this could also be a good candidate algorithm to explore for the CharityML problem. Since our problem includes more than just categorical data, we'll need to leverage a variant of the base Naive Bayes Classifier, Gaussian Naive Bayes, making the assumptions that our continuous data, e.g. age, capital-gain, etc. adheres to a normal distribution.

One of the strengths of Naive Bayes classifiers is that they are fast. Because the algorithm assumes conditional independence, it only requires calculation of the probability of each feature, which can be done quite quickly. For the same reason, it scales well to problems with many dimensions, largely avoiding the curse of dimensionality.

As for weaknesses, while it is a good classifier, it's a bad estimator, so while it might be good at classifing an individual as making more or less than \$50k/year, it's not as good a predicting how much they make. Additionally, it is making the strong assumption that our conditions are independent. While this has historically not proven to be a bad assumption, in cases where there are strong dependence between conditions, I could see this significantly impacting the results. Finally, since we're going to be using Gaussian Naive Bayes to deal with our continuous data, if those conditions don't follow a normal distribution, this could also be a weak performer for a problem like this.

In the real-world, the primary usage of Naive Bayes classifiers is in spam detection, text classification, e.g. is this news artical about sports or technology, as well as medical diagnosis.

from sklearn.metrics import fbeta_score, accuracy_score

def train_predict(learner, sample_size, X_train, y_train, X_test, y_test): 
    '''
    inputs:
       - learner: the learning algorithm to be trained and predicted on
       - sample_size: the size of samples (number) to be drawn from training set
       - X_train: features training set
       - y_train: income training set
       - X_test: features testing set
       - y_test: income testing set
    '''
    
    results = {}
    
    # TODO: Fit the learner to the training data using slicing with 'sample_size'
    start = time() # Get start time
    learner.fit(X_train[:sample_size], y_train[:sample_size])
    end = time() # Get end time
    
    # TODO: Calculate the training time
    results['train_time'] = end - start
        
    # TODO: Get the predictions on the test set,
    #       then get predictions on the first 300 training samples
    start = time() # Get start time
    predictions_test = learner.predict(X_test)
    predictions_train = learner.predict(X_train[:300])
    end = time() # Get end time
    
    # TODO: Calculate the total prediction time
    results['pred_time'] = end - start
            
    # TODO: Compute accuracy on the first 300 training samples
    results['acc_train'] = accuracy_score(y_train[:300], predictions_train)
        
    # TODO: Compute accuracy on test set
    results['acc_test'] = accuracy_score(y_test, predictions_test)
    
    # TODO: Compute F-score on the the first 300 training samples
    results['f_train'] = fbeta_score(y_train[:300], predictions_train, beta=0.5)
        
    # TODO: Compute F-score on the test set
    results['f_test'] = fbeta_score(y_test, predictions_test, beta=0.5)
       
    # Success
    print "{} trained on {} samples.".format(learner.__class__.__name__, sample_size)
        
    # Return the results
    return results

As described above, we're choosing the AdaBoost Classifier to help us classify the people in your dataset into two groups, those that make no more than \$50k/year and those that make more than \$50k/year. AdaBoost is short for "Adaptive Boosting" and is a type of learning algorithm called ensemble methods, which all basically combine the results of multiple learners to arrive at a final result.

The way AdaBoost works is by combining a set of simpler learning algorithms, called weak learners—which really just means any learning algorithm with less than a 50% error rate, or better than a random guess in a binary classification problem—and iteriatively focusses the subsequent learners on the items that the previous learners classified incorrectly. This is why it is considered adaptive. Finally, it takes a weighted sum of the predictions of each of the weak learners to arrive at the final prediction, with more accurate intermediate models having a higher weight.
